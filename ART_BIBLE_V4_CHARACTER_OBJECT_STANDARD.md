# Art Bible V4 — Characters and Interactive Objects

> Character silhouettes and clothing are superseded by `ART_BIBLE_V5_CHARACTER_DIRECTION.md`. Object, interaction, motion and environmental-response rules in this document remain active.

## Status

This is a locked production standard for the existing wallet encounter and every interaction added after it. It is based on:

- `output/pdf/the-garden-remembers-art-bible-v4-characters.pdf`
- `output/pdf/the-garden-remembers-art-bible-v4-wallet-encounter.pdf`
- The full-resolution before/after character and encounter cuts in `assets/art_bible/characters_v1/`

The cuts are references for real-time composition, material, proportion, light and state change. They must never be pasted into the experience as backgrounds, sprites or scenic plates.

## Core rule

An interaction is not an animated object surrounded by unchanged scenery. It is a state change across one continuous composition:

> character + held object + path + water + planting + paper + nearby props + light

The same people, props and layout remain present before and after a choice. Meaning appears through pose, gaze, material response, reflected pigment, movement and environmental memory.

## Character construction

### Required silhouette

- Adult, calm human proportions; approximately 7–8 heads tall.
- A small head relative to the body, narrow shoulders and a long layered silhouette.
- Hood, cloak or outer robe constructed as hanging cloth planes with believable weight and folds.
- Loose lower garments and grounded footwear; feet must feel planted on stone.
- Hands remain restrained and anatomically scaled. They become visible only when a gesture needs them.
- The silhouette must read at a distance before facial detail is visible.

### Before interaction

- Body turned partly away or held in profile.
- Gaze lowered or directed away from the player.
- Closed pose: arms contained by the garment, shoulders slightly gathered, weight quiet.
- Indigo costume remains the strongest local value shape.
- Emotion is implied by stillness, distance and posture—not performed at the player.

### After interaction

- Preserve the same identity, costume, proportions and position in the composition.
- Turn the torso and gaze toward the player gradually.
- Open one hand with a small, credible gesture; use the other hand to receive or hold the object where relevant.
- Allow warm reflected color to enter the face and cloth without recoloring the costume wholesale.
- Let the response continue into water, paper, plants, path and light so the character is not the only reacting element.

### Forbidden character language

- No oversized spherical head, mitten hands or capsule limbs.
- No emoji eyes, drawn mouth, visible tear drops, bouncing head or repeated sob bob.
- No facial caricature, theatrical acting or exaggerated gratitude.
- No costume swap to indicate a resolved state.
- No UI emote, speech bubble or glow ring standing in for acting.
- No response that exists only on the character.

The current procedural crying figure violates these rules and must be replaced, not merely given more subdivisions or surface detail.

## Interactive-object family

All focal objects must look as if they were made for the same garden. Their detail comes from construction, material edges, contact with the ground and evidence of use—not from adding decorative geometry.

### Shared material palette

- Pale limestone: subtle pores, irregular softened edges, moss or leaf-shadow variation.
- Handmade paper: warm ivory, visible fibers, translucent depth and pigment collecting at edges.
- Weathered wood: quiet grain, rounded touch points, no glossy varnish.
- Aged metal: restrained bronze, brass or indigo enamel; localized wear only where handled.
- Ceramic: mineral variation and soft imperfect rims, never uniform plastic gloss.
- Textile: woven roughness, weighted folds and slow wind response.
- Water: shallow and legible over stone, with refraction and broad pigment transport.

### Shared shape language

- Human-scale, slender and composed rather than chunky or toy-like.
- Softened corners and sculptural asymmetry, but no random wobble.
- Clear primary silhouette, one or two secondary construction details and quiet tertiary texture.
- Objects contact or belong to architecture; avoid unrelated freestanding stations.
- Negative space around each choice is part of its readability.

### Shared interaction language

- All available choices receive equal visual invitation.
- Invitation comes from material movement, reflected light, sound source and composition—not identical sparkle emitters.
- Close-range text may clarify the situation, but the object must communicate its function before the prompt appears.
- Activating an object produces a physical cause first, followed by a broad environmental response.
- The response remains as a quieter persistent state after the animation ends.
- Objects do not spawn, swap models or change location to indicate resolution.

## Motion standard

- Use graceful acceleration and long settling tails; no popping or mechanical snapping.
- Character movement begins in weight transfer, then torso, gaze, arm and hand.
- Cloth follows the body with restrained secondary motion.
- Object activation follows cause → propagation → recognition → settled trace.
- Environmental response uses broad pigment fronts and connected surfaces, not isolated particle bursts.
- Reduced-motion mode preserves the state change with shorter dissolves and material transitions.

## Camera and scale checks

- Judge the character first from normal first-person viewing distance, not from an isolated close-up.
- The player’s eye height should meet the character around eye or upper-face level.
- Keep enough negative space around the figure for the before/after pose to read.
- Props must be recognizable from their approach view and remain correctly scaled beside the character.
- Foreground planting or paper may frame a view but must not obscure interaction targets or paths.

## Approval gates

An interactive asset is not approved unless all are true:

1. Its silhouette communicates category and state without a label.
2. It shares the garden’s limestone, paper, wood, ceramic, textile or aged-metal material language.
3. It remains in the same place before and after interaction.
4. Its response begins with an understandable physical cause.
5. Its response reaches at least two surrounding environmental systems.
6. A quieter trace remains after the main animation.
7. It does not rely on cartoon acting, a generic glow, a floating icon or isolated particles.
8. It stays luminous, painterly and legible in the normal first-person camera.

The wallet encounter must pass these gates before visual production begins on the threshold encounter.
